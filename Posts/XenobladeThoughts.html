Thoughts on Xenoblade Chronicles X

I wasn't a fan of the original Xenoblade, and a lot of the issues with that game have carried over into the sequel. The combat is too simple, the characters are built atop a mountain of tropes, and the multitude of quests carry the same tedium you'd commonly find in an MMO. Despite all of that, the 70 hours I poured into this game passed by in a blink. Even with the main story completed, it's still a joy to freely roam around the gorgeous environments populated across Mira. The game's world is filled to the brim with secrets. Of all that could be said about it, this game nails the formula of stumbling into an area and gradually depleting the supply of treasure from its every knook and cranny.

In my mind, the changes done to player movement have allowed X to succeed where the original failed. Your character does an instant zero to sixty the moment you hit the sprint button and can leap high enough to scale a three story building. This struck me as a bit comical the first time I saw it, especially for my character who adorns the sad clown makeup as his war paint.

Bojangles dresses for every battle as if it were his last.

Yet there's no denying how amazingly fluid this feels. Every point of interest spotted way off in the horizon can be reached in a matter of seconds. This holds true for scaling large structures as well. Many of the walls and cliffsides seen in the environment contain small outcroppings of footholds that can be jumped on, turning areas that seem tantalizingly out of reach into puzzles waiting to be solved. This makes it all too easy to deviate from the path leading to the main objective and chase down the endless rabbit hole that comprises this game's amount of optional content.

But as much as I love getting sidetracked, I eventually found my way back to the main questline. This is where the game loses a lot of its steam. The plot itself is interesting enough: the remnants of human society have crash landed on an alien planet where the scarcity of resources has turned every thought toward survival. It's just the delivery of this narrative that puts me to sleep. Between the recycled jokes and stale dialogue, I can't think of a single character who stands out strongly enough to be worth mentioning. On top of that, the urgency in the world never feels reflected in the interactions between characters. The clock could be ticking down toward some doomsday event; but instead of a frenzied panic, dialogue is spoken with the same level of robotic calmness that's ever-present throughout the entirety of the game.

Though it's not all doom and gloom. The combat and story might not be the best in the industry, but Xenoblade Chronicles X is easily one of the best titles around when it comes to exploration. I can't recommend this game enough if you have a heart that loves to wonder. Getting lost in the expansiveness of a virtual world is one of the unique gifts the medium of games has to offer, and this box is definitely worth unwrapping. You just have to be mindful of the rough edges when investing yourself into the enormous world of Mira.
